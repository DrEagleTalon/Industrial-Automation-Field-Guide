Voltage Drop – Planning and Testing

Voltage Drop - Planning and Testing

Description

Voltage drop is the reduction in voltage between power source and load due to conductor resistance and circuit impedance. This guide explains why voltage drop matters, how to estimate it during design, and how to verify it during commissioning and troubleshooting.

Core Concepts

What voltage drop means

Every wire has resistance. As current flows, some source voltage is lost across the conductor. If too much is lost, equipment at the end of the run sees low voltage and may operate poorly or fail to start.

Why it matters in industrial systems

  • Motors draw higher current at low voltage and may overheat.
  • Contactors may chatter if coil voltage dips too far.
  • Drives and power supplies can trip on undervoltage faults.
  • Sensors and control circuits can become unstable.

What drives voltage drop

  • Long cable length.
  • High load current.
  • Small conductor cross section.
  • Higher conductor temperature.
  • Poor terminations adding contact resistance.

Planning Guidelines

Circuit TypePractical TargetReason
Feeder circuitsKeep low, often around 2-3%Reserve margin for downstream branch circuits
Branch power circuitsCommon target 3%Supports motor and heater performance
Total feeder + branchCommon design target 5% maxGeneral good practice for utilization voltage
Control circuitsTighter limits where coil dropout is criticalPrevents relay chatter and control faults

Project standards and local code requirements take priority. Use them first, then apply these planning targets.

Quick Estimation Workflow

  1. Determine circuit current at normal and worst-case load.
  2. Determine one-way conductor length and total loop path.
  3. Select preliminary conductor size and material.
  4. Use accepted voltage drop method for single-phase or three-phase circuit.
  5. Compare estimated drop to your design target.
  6. Upsize conductors or shorten route if target is not met.

Important: Motor starting conditions can produce larger temporary drops than steady-state calculations.

Field Testing Method

Steady load test

  • Measure voltage at source under load.
  • Measure voltage at load terminals at the same time if possible.
  • Subtract to find actual drop at current operating condition.

Startup test

  • Capture minimum voltage during motor or high-inrush startup.
  • Compare with equipment undervoltage thresholds.
  • Correlate with inrush current and protective device behavior.

Connection integrity check

  • Perform thermal scan under load to detect high-resistance joints.
  • Retorque and retest where elevated temperature is found.

Field Checklist

  • Confirm conductor size against circuit current and run length.
  • Measure loaded voltage at source and at end device.
  • Record startup minimum voltage for major motor loads.
  • Inspect and torque critical terminations.
  • Check if cable tray or conduit route can be shortened in redesign.
  • Document measured drop percentage for future baseline comparison.
